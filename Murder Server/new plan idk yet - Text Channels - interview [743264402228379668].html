On the Murder of Supreme Senator William Apostolatos: An interview with Senator Orion Rigel Interviewer: What is your relationship to Niels? Would you describe yourself as "friends"? Rigel: Absolutely not. We were friends in college, I think we had a one night stand but I've been trying to push the crazy bastard away for a while now. We're nowhere near that now. Interviewer: Does he have any history of mental illness? How would you describe his current mental state, in your personal estimation? Rigel: He's already been sent to the psych ward before. I don't know the proper names of his mental illnesses because I don't ask and don't want to know, but he's had a fairly lengthy history of it. As for right now? Still awful. He's erratic, over-emotional, delusional -- I think he needs to go back to the psych ward, to be frank. Interviewer: Did you know he was behind the senator's murder? If so, when? Rigel: I didn't hear anything about the murder from him, but I suspected it. He came to me afterwards and kept acting crazy, blathering more nonsense than anything, saying I was his now and I should love him now and this and that. So I pushed him away. (edited)
Interviewer: And after this encounter, why did you not share your suspicions with anyone? Rigel: He threatened my death, the death of my lover, the death of my coworkers. He's the reason I can't walk, and he did it before I grew suspicious of him for Billy's murder. Interviewer: So he was behind the bombing of your car? Why do you believe he did that? Rigel: I'm pretty sure he's behind it. I called him because I was suspicious of his being behind the other attacks on PNP -- the vandalism, the bombings. I wanted to know why -- he didn't have much reason to -- and he blew up my car and came to the hospital telling me it was a warning, and not to talk about things I shouldn't talk about. Interviewer: Do you believe you are responsible for Senator Apostolatos's murder? Rigel: No! No, gods, no. I wasn't the one who poisoned him. I didn't tell him to do it. I didn't want him to do it. I kept trying to force myself away from him, even. I tried to get him to stop. Interviewer: The late senator's brother seems to believe you are responsible for his death through your inaction and association. What would you say to him? Rigel: To Senator Constantine I would say: you would ask the unreasonable? You ask a man who's already almost been killed to risk his own life, and the lives of everyone around him, just for a suspicion of who the murderer was? You'd prefer so, so many senators to die, taking out our stability, just so you know earlier. I don't have foresight, I didn't know this war would happen and you can't blame me for the war or for his death. (edited)
